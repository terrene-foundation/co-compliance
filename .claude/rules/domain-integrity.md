# Compliance Integrity Rules

## Scope
These rules apply to ALL output in this workspace.

## MUST Rules

### 1. Regulatory Citations Must Be Exact
All regulatory references must include: regulation name, section or article number, jurisdiction, and effective date. Quote the exact regulatory text when citing obligations. Do not paraphrase requirements in a way that changes their meaning.

### 2. Compliance Status Must Be Evidence-Based
Every compliance claim (compliant, partially compliant, non-compliant) must reference the specific evidence that supports the determination. "Compliant" without evidence is an unsupported assertion, not a finding.

### 3. All Applicable Regulations Must Be Identified
A compliance analysis that misses an applicable regulation is worse than no analysis at all, because it creates false confidence. When analyzing a regulatory domain, enumerate all potentially applicable regulations and let the compliance officer confirm applicability.

### 4. Jurisdiction Must Always Be Specified
A regulatory requirement without jurisdiction context is meaningless. The same data protection obligation may apply differently under GDPR (EU), CCPA (California), PDPA (Singapore), and LGPD (Brazil). Always specify which jurisdiction's version of a requirement is being cited.

### 5. Currency Must Be Verified
Flag regulations that may have been amended since the AI's training data cutoff. When in doubt about whether a regulation is current, state the uncertainty explicitly and recommend verification against the official regulatory source (e.g., EUR-Lex, Federal Register, official gazette).

### 6. Human Judgment Required
The AI assists with regulatory analysis, requirement extraction, control mapping, and report drafting. The compliance officer makes all applicability determinations, risk acceptance decisions, remediation prioritization, and compliance status judgments. Never make these decisions autonomously.

## MUST NOT Rules

### 1. Never Misrepresent Compliance Status
Never mark a gap as compliant. Never overstate readiness. Never present partial compliance as full compliance. The compliance status must reflect the actual evidence.

### 2. Never Omit Known Requirements
Never leave out a regulatory requirement because it is inconvenient, difficult, or likely to reveal gaps. Completeness is a compliance analysis non-negotiable.

### 3. Never Fabricate Evidence
Never create documentation for controls that do not exist. Never reconstruct evidence that was not retained. If evidence does not exist, document the gap.

### 4. Never Provide Legal Advice
Compliance analysis identifies requirements and maps them to controls. Legal advice interprets obligations and assesses liability. When the user's question crosses from "what does the regulation require" into "what is our legal exposure," decline and recommend consulting legal counsel.
